Catholic alphabet

I present forms from various works written in the Catholic alphabet. That's how I personally call it, I'm unaware of how it's usually labelled by scholars. The works are
  • Gjon Buzuku (1555), Meshari, manuscript in the Vatican Library
  • Pjetër Budi (1618), Dottrina Christiana, Rome: B. Zannetti
  • Frang Bardhi (1635), Dictionarium Latino-Epiroticum, Rome: Propaganda Fide
  • Pjetër Budi (1664), Dottrina Christiana (2nd ed), Rome: Propaganda Fide
  • Pjetër Bogdani (1685), Cuneus Prophetarum, Padova: Tipografia del Seminario
  • A letter from the elders of Gashi directed to the Pope (1689)
  • Kuvendi i Arbënit (1706), Rome: Propaganda Fide
  • Giuseppe Guagliata (1856), Dottrina e Kërshten, Rome: Propaganda Fide
  • Vincenzo Basil (1873), Rruga e Parrisit, Rome: Propaganda Fide
  • Giacomo Jungg (1887), Kulshedra e shpirtit, Shkodër
  • Pesmdhet biseda t' Shejtit sh' Leonard prej Porto Maurizio (1900), Shkodër: Typ. Immacolatae Concept. B. M. V.
  • Smuetorja e Austri-Hungaris (1904), Shkodër
They all use more or less the same orthography, with the exception of Buzuku, which has more symbols (see below) and doesn't use Italian digraphs. The letter ⟨λ⟩ is introduced by Bogdani, earlier works use ⟨l⟩ or ⟨ll⟩. The first work to show casing variants for these letters is the Kuvendi.

Remarks on encoding

We can encode ll as Greek Λ λ, y as Latin Ȣ ȣ, and z as Latin Ɛ ɛ, but while dh is currently often transcribed as Greek Ξ ξ, that's a pretty long stretch both semantically and visually. I propose the addition of the codepoints LATIN SMALL LETTER OLD ALBANIAN DH and its counterpart LATIN CAPITAL LETTER OLD ALBANIAN DH. I also dislike using a character called OPEN E for the sound z, so I would have also suggested LATIN SMALL/CAPITAL LETTER OLD ALBANIAN Z. Although publishing houses used their existing woodcut of the Greek epsilon, the letter most likely descends from a cursive form of ζ. The image you can see on the right, for example, is how ζ is written in the 1834 manuscript Dottrina a Krështe from San Basile (not Catholic script, merely a Latin with Greek letters).
