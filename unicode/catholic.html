Catholic alphabet

I present forms from various works written in the Catholic alphabet, also known as Old Shkodran. The works are
  • Gjon Buzuku (1555), Meshari, manuscript in the Vatican Library
  • Pjetër Budi (1618), Dottrina Christiana, Rome: B. Zannetti
  • Frang Bardhi (1635), Dictionarium Latino-Epiroticum, Rome: Propaganda Fide
  • Pjetër Budi (1664), Dottrina Christiana (2nd ed), Rome: Propaganda Fide
  • Pjetër Bogdani (1685), Cuneus Prophetarum, Padova: Tipografia del Seminario
  • A letter from the elders of Gashi directed to the Pope (1689)
  • Kuvendi i Arbënit (1706), Rome: Propaganda Fide
  • Giuseppe Guagliata (1856), Dottrina e Kërshten, Rome: Propaganda Fide
  • Vincenzo Basile (1873), Rruga e Parrisit, Rome: Propaganda Fide
  • Giacomo Jungg (1887), Kulshedra e shpirtit, Shkodër
  • Pesmdhet biseda t' Shejtit sh' Leonard prej Porto Maurizio (1900), Shkodër: Typ. Immacolatae Concept. B. M. V.
  • Smuetorja e Austri-Hungaris (1904), Shkodër
Buzuku's (1555) orthography cannot be categorised as Catholic alphabet, as unlike the others it doesn't use Italian digraphs and has some more symbols. However the Catholic alphabet inherited its letters from this northern Albanian tradition (of which no other work aside from Buzuku survives), so from an encoding perspective they are treated together. The later works use more or less the same orthography, with varying choices on how to spell ë (always however a relatively basic Latin character already in Unicode, hence not treated here). The letter ⟨λ⟩ is introduced by Bogdani (1685), earlier works use ⟨l⟩ or ⟨ll⟩. The first work to show casing variants for these letters is the Kuvendi (1706). The reason why some letters forms are not in the table is for linguistical reasons, not typographical, y and ll are very rare word-initially, so I couldn't find them in the works.

Remarks on encoding

We can encode ll as Greek Λ λ, y as Latin Ȣ ȣ, and z as Latin Ɛ ɛ, but while dh is currently often transcribed as Greek Ξ ξ, that's a pretty long stretch both semantically and visually. I propose the addition of new codepoints for it. I also propose to add annotation to the existing LATIN SMALL LETTER OPEN E and its capital form mentioning its use as Albanian z.

Secondary sources

  • Stavro Skendi (1960), History of the Albanian Alphabet: A Case of Complex Cultural and Political Development, in Südost-Forschungen, 29, Munich, p. 264f.:
    But Buzuku's alphabet does not seem to have had followers, for in Pjetër Budi's (1566–1623) works, as for instance in the Christian Doctrine (1618), we meet a different script, although founded on the Latin one. Budi's alphabet, with slight changes, was employed in the Dictionary (1635) by Frang Bardhi and in Cuneus Prophetarum (1685) by Pjetër Bogdani. It was preserved until the outset of the 20th century by the Jesuit priests of Shkodër, who continued to publish in it. This system is usually called "the Alphabet of the Ancient Writers of the North", for it was used by them; it is also referred to at times as "the Catholic Alphabet", because only the Catholic part of the Albanian population wrote in it. It remained, however, restricted to the province of Shkodër.
